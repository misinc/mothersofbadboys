Home School, Part 1

Saturday, October 17, 2009

By Denny Mather

Moving to Montana

 

I'd thought of starting my "bad boy" saga with the difficult pregnancy followed by a caesarian but realized that would be going back too far and quite a few people might believe I was being serious. So, skipping ahead many years I'll concentrate on why home schooling was our only choice.

 

Vincent, my son, was in the public school system from kindergarten through fifth grade. He'd had a few problems but we were able to resolve them. I was able to go to his various classrooms one day a week (doing whatever his teacher at the time needed: grading papers, helping with coloring, being playground monitor, tutoring, etc.) so I had a good relationship with teachers, kids, and some administrators. I could see most problems before they got bad and would talk with teachers about them.

 

That all changed when we moved from northern California to Sumtown, Montana, the summer before Vincent was going to be in sixth grade. My husband had a new job as a field wildlife biologist. He traveled almost 1000 miles a month to different sites around the state and was gone for 11 days at a time. We bought a house four blocks away from an elementary school and anticipated the start of school eagerly. Vincent and I walked there several times over the summer to look at it and wonder if any of the kids he would meet would live close by.

 

Registration started in August and I was there the first day with paperwork from California schools and expressed my desire to help in the classroom. The principal, Mr. X, told me that I would need to have a background check before they could allow me on school property. I handed him letters of reference from previous teachers and asked what I would need to do for a background check. He hemmed and hawed and said he would let me know.

 

Sumtown schools have sixth grade set up to be as much like middle school as possible, different classrooms and teachers for each subject. Mr. X said it was to get the kids used to the idea so it wouldn't be such a shock to them when they moved on to a bigger, more crowded school. (I think it's a waste of time. Rather than learning something, the kids are waiting for one class to be over and goofing off as much as possible moving on to the next room.) 

 

The first day of school finally arrived. I walked Vincent up the hill and wished him well at the school gates. I spent the next hours cleaning and puttering and enjoying the peace and quiet. At three, I walked back up the hill and met Vinnie at the gate. He wasn't happy at all and had nothing good to say about anyone or anything. One of his teachers had introduced him to the class as a California kid and went on to say that Montana children had fewer problems with discipline and that Vincent would have to work real hard to "prove" that he could be as good as the rest of them. Vincent also had a hard time finding his way from room to room and no one bothered to help guide him around.

 

I comforted him as best I could. Cookies and platitudes really don't go very far sometimes.

 

Vincent would have music on the following day and was required to bring in cans, small chains, rubber bands and the like. Noisemakers, yes. Music?? I don't think so.  Luckily, I had plenty of such junk around and we easily gathered enough for him to have plenty of variety.
