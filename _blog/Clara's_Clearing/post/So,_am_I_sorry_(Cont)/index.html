So, am I sorry? (Cont.)

Friday, January 15, 2010

My first reservation about my son’s private school was how joyless the environment was and how subdued and brow-beaten the children looked. Equally disturbing, however, were things that were more social and political in nature. From what I saw at that school I learned how deeply discriminatory private schools have become.


Doing my share of volunteer work at the school and sitting on various committees I became more and more bothered by the “screening” that goes on in private school admissions. First of all, the exorbitant tuitions – between $15,000 and $20,000 in our area – conveniently weed out all kinds of “undesirable” people. I mean, if you can’t swallow that kind of tuition, you automatically don’t belong to “us” kind of folks. Of course all these schools claim that financial ability does not enter into the admission decision – but I wonder if there’s anybody out there who actually believes this! So… poor and even middle class people are already weeded out.


Then comes the question of “diversity.” You look at any private school brochure and they make sure they casually put in some dark faces in their publicity pictures. First of all, as if any dark face – African American, Latino, Indian – proves that the school does not discriminate on the basis of financial need. There are lots of darker-skinned people with money, you know. What really bothered me, however, was listening to all the talk about “recruiting” “diversity” students. I really disliked the use of the word “recruit” while talking about admission policy. I mean, we never talked about “recruiting” rich white kids, did we? And anyway, why would we need to “recruit” minorities (who are not strictly speaking necessarily minority in numbers where we live!)? If our doors were really open to everybody in our community we would not be talking about “recruiting” from certain groups, would we?


The reason we talked about “recruiting” and not “accepting” was the question of screening. We didn’t want just any kind of “diversity.” We wanted certain kinds of people – “of color” or not. In fact, we were using the way people look to prove that we were open-minded and didn’t care how people looked! To me, it proved that the school’s notion of diversity was the appearance of diversity, as if people who look different necessarily are different. You get my point? Just because someone has a darker skin color doesn’t mean that they are fundamentally different from someone with a lighter skin color. I thought the school was not only hypocritical but actually betrayed the very prejudice it was trying to prove it didn’t have. (And I won’t even go into how we didn’t even much try to recruit teachers of color. People of color in actual positions of authority and as role models…? Oh no!!!)


So exactly how “diverse” were we?


We were hardly diverse – unless a lot of people were in camouflage. What I saw was that everybody tried very hard to talk a certain talk and walk a certain walk.


The point is that, ultimately, what makes people diverse is not skin color or income. It’s the kind of people they are. My problem with the way most private schools operate is that they screen for particular kinds of people. They are discriminatory based on personal and cultural traits that go much deeper than skin color. They pick people who can be molded in a particular way. They value pliability and ability to conform. They like people who don’t challenge and don’t make trouble. They want uniformity of personality and certain particular values.


In my family we don’t have moldable personality types, and conformity and docility are not our values. And as I found out, the natural diversity in public schools has no choice but to make room for odd people like us and our kind of values. Our family mixed in much better at our public school. We met many more interesting people than we had in the private school.


But as I’ve been writing in my previous blogs, we also found out that battling the troubles in public schools is not pretty or easy. Even when these battles don’t destroy you they leave their scars. Yes, if my son’s public school had more money to have better yard supervision a lot of the bullying that he was subjected to would not have happened. If the school was really able to demand better teaching from its teachers all the kids would benefit enormously. Of course this is true. And of course it is true that private schools do have better yard supervision, have more freedom in dealing with repeat bullies, and do attract some very good teachers (who don’t want to spend a lot of energy coping with public school shortcomings). Perhaps Jack would have had a better time at the private school: he would not have been harassed by the second grade teacher and would not have been bullied to the extent that he was.


So I ask myself, Am I sorry I took my kid out of private school and put him in public?
