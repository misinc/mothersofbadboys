Manners and Morals

Manners, Culture, Beauty

Tuesday, March 15, 2011

By Pendar


I grew up in Iran. Iran has an old culture and old cultures have complex and complicated social norms. Let me disclose something. People from old Eastern cultures (say Iran, China or India) think people from the West can never quite master this complexity. In the eyes of the East the West is awfully crude. And of course we know how the East fares in the eyes of the West: distasteful, corrupt, and one step away from barbarity.


Now, in Iran people are far from barbarous. In fact social interactions can be crushingly formal. After years of being away from Iran I sometimes catch myself making hideous faux-pas: taking an offer literally, forgetting to inquire after the health of every family member, stretching my legs in front of an older person. And what makes it really hard is that you’re not supposed to act stiff or show that you are consciously observing manners. It should all just pour out of you naturally. Good form always looks completely natural.


Manners are tricky business and the training starts in childhood. Obviously very small children begin with observing. They see that no matter what chaos is reigning in the household the minute company arrives everybody straightens out. In fact, space is arranged in observation of manners. The living room, reserved for company, is where you don’t take your bad mood or bad behavior with you. And no matter how poor the household you sell the shirt off your back to bring good food to the table for your guest.


It’s not just about hospitality either. Strict forms apply to treatment of seniority and superiority (age and social class) and distance (the in-laws versus the blood relatives). You instinctively come to each other’s aid in observing manners. Still to this day, after decades of being away from Iran for many of us, I know that I can count on my Iranian friends to come to my aid in awkward situations instinctively. Without exchanging a glance we can read each other’s minds and prop each other up till the awkward situation is brought under form control. One of the things kids learn early on is this family/social reliance. It’s almost as if suddenly a lights/camera/action signal goes off and you all perform together. Children learn early on to take to this stage.


Now, it is absolutely true that when you master such intricate social performance you can become a consummate liar. You can easily pretend to be and think things you are not and do not. In the west this is seen as rather sociopathic. And indeed it sometimes is. But, when it is not done to hurt and deceive others, it is an aesthetic choice. It can be sublime refinement. Or it can just simply be a considerate and beautiful way to behave with others.


To many of us observing good manners is like brining a blooming pot of fragrant hyacinths into a room. It is an aesthetically pleasing addition to social interaction. And more than kids learning a strict code of conduct what they learn is appreciation for this aesthetic. You’ve seen a Japanese tea ceremony? It’s a meditation but also just engaging in something very pleasing to the senses. It’s like performing a beautiful painting.


I’m not saying kids in the west don’t learn manners. Even now, at a very low period for manners in the United States, children still learn something to the effect of not pouncing on the food at the dinner table or occasionally shaking hands with someone. In Europe they learn a lot more. And certainly at the height of highly codified manners – say, the public face of British high society in the past few centuries – children learn social performance early on and the formal codes trickle down to the not-so-high society. The culture adopts a certain aesthetic.


To western ears music from eastern cultures – again, think Chinese, Indian, Iranian, Arab – sounds out of tune and monotonous. To early Iranian visitors to Europe the little tuning interval of an orchestra sounded no different from the actual symphony. In fact, there is the travelogue of a certain Iranian Shah who actually preferred the tuning to the concert. The same can be true of manners. The East can laugh at the West and vice versa.


And yet… how is it that in spite of radical aesthetic differences and inaccessible cultural codes, there are people from both sides who can not only conduct themselves very well when placed in the other context but in fact appreciate and even successfully evaluate performance in the other system? I think because at the heart of it, manners are not just a question of respect and consideration for others but just plain old love of the beautiful.


Just like respect for others begins witih respect for respect itself, behaving in a pleasing manner begins with just love of beauty – not one particular kind of beauty but beauty of any kind. Rudyard Kipling said East is East and West is West and never the twain shall meet. I think the twain can indeed meet in mutual recognition of the love of beauty. And observing good manners -- behaving beautifully -- begins with the recognition of beauty at the heart of the question of manners.
