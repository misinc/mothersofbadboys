Manners and Morals

Respect, Formality, and Manners

Tuesday, February 15, 2011

By Pendar


One of the classic complaints against teenagers in this country is that they don’t show respect. And indeed they don’t show respect because they don’t have respect. I think this is partly because a lot of adults in their lives haven’t proved themselves worthy of respect, but also because the kids are not taught to demonstrate respect. In fact, a lot of what gets called not having respect is not having manners. Manners are the demonstration of respect.
 

In the old days – and in many cultures even now – when the teacher enters a classroom, for instance, the children pay some kind of respect. Where I grew up the class rose when the teacher entered the classroom. The children hushed their noise and then took their seats quietly. If the teacher was respectful to the children he or she would greet the children warmly and with dignity. And if the teacher was a jerk he or she would act with arrogance and disdain. In fact, it was the teacher’s manners that first said something about him or her to the students. Nevertheless, manners were observed. The older the students got the more aware they became of whether a teacher deserved respect or not – and it’s definitely true that not all adults, including teachers, deserve a whole lot of respect – but a certain level of formal behavior was observed regardless.


Manners inevitably involve a degree of formality. We did not talk to our teachers or our elders in general, the same way we talked to our friends. We never called adults by their first names. In languages where the second person has a singular and plural form (for example, in French, tu and vous), it is still not acceptable for younger people to refer to older people in second person singular. Formality does acknowledge a certain level of distance between people.


When I taught college in the US my students were by far mostly non-white and immigrant. Having been introduced to American informality, when I first started teaching I would ask my students to call me by my first name. They vehemently objected. They called me Professor. They would explain to me something to the effect of, “Why would we work so hard to come to college if we wanted to be taught by a Tom, Dick, or Mary?” I had a wonderful, close and highly rewarding relationship with my students. We would meet outside school, go to museums, roam the city together, and often joke with one another. But I was always “professor.” Even when they said the word in a teasing or sassy way, I was still “professor.” A certain distance was always maintained between us, even though in age we were not that far apart. And, oddly enough, that distance gave us all a sense of freedom -- it's a bit hard to explain!


In America, especially in the white culture, formality is frowned upon. It is seen as “undemocratic,” or even “elitist.” In America we like to pretend there are no hierarchies: everybody is on equal footing at all times and there is no difference between them. We say “everybody’s entitled to their opinion” and we consider all “opinions” equal: whether it is the informed and measured thought of an educated and experienced person or the media-induced knee-jerk reaction of Joe Blow on the street or his gum-snapping teenage daughter. It is “elitist” to say that the educated person probably knows what he or she is talking about more than Joe Blow and his daughter. And yet, when that educated person as a teacher walks into the classroom and the teenagers continue to snap their gums at him or her, we are shocked that “kids nowadays have no respect.”


So, on the one hand we pretend we are all equal in this country and on the other hand we complain that legitimate authority is not distinguished, hence respected. And when the kids’ behavior reflects this hypocrisy we freak out and call the children names. We throw labels at them: they have "conduct disorder," they are ODD (Oppositional Defiant Disorder), or whatnot. We don’t see that we are giving them mixed messages.


Now, I would say one way to remedy this situation is to observe some manners. I think a certain degree of formality is OK. I think acknowledging legitimate hierarchy is honest and necessary. But even when respect is not necessarily earned – as in the case of many a Joe Blow as well as Professor Schmow – still it doesn’t hurt for the teenager not to feel free to turn his or her back to the teacher in class or spit in the street. This may only be a start – or some may object that it’s superficial and a hypocritical start – but how can we get anywhere without at least a start?

I am pleased that in most schools even in the U.S. children still address their teachers as Ms. or Mr. So-and-So. I understand this puts a little distance between children and adults but, let’s be honest, there is a distance between children and adults. The formal manifestation of this distance is through observing manners. And the demonstration of manners is a recognition of respect: Even when the respect is not necessarily for the object of respect it is still a demonstration of respect for respect itself.
