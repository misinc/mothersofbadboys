Manners and Morals

Impulse Control and Manners

Saturday, January 15, 2011

By Pendar


One of the charges against a lot of kids, especially boys, nowadays is that they don’t have “impulse control.” It is one of those non-official diagnoses that are often passed among teachers and school administrators until they reach mental health professionals and they become official. In the hand of professionals, “impulse control” takes on all kinds of dimensions: from neurological to personality deficiencies.  


But what exactly is impulse control?


This question has been with us a lot longer than the field of psychology. All major religions are in fact based on prescriptions for controlling human impulses. Think of the Ten Commandments of Moses. Think of the idea of resisting temptation in Christianity, and the whole concept of sin. The pieties and privations in Islam are also aimed at overcoming the self and its ceaseless demands. It is easy to see how in these religions the question of morality is tied into the notion of controlling “base” human impulses.


In other religions and spiritual traditions, controlling impulse is also central. In fact, not giving in to impulse is what meditation – observing the “monkey mind” – is all about: watching the impulse arise and pass. In Buddhism there is the analogy of taming the ox for controlling the mind. And the word Yoga is derived from the Sanskrit word that exists in English as “yoke” – the idea is that you need to put a yoke on your chaotic mind whose impulses are constantly throwing you in every which direction.
 

Unreligious as Freud was, he also thought that controlling our wild nature (Id) and its impulsive demands  for pleasure (the “pleasure principle”) is the first step in the development of our sense of self (Ego). Delaying gratification and sublimation are certainly forms of impulse control. In fact, Freud saw impulse control at the very heart of human civilization.


The list goes on and on. In every culture and in every historical period people have strained to the utmost trying to understand what these potentially uncontrollable impulses of human beings are and how to control them. It certainly does not take a messenger from God or a genius to see that children are not born with impulse control. But it certainly has taken quite a few messengers from God and geniuses to remind us that learning to control impulses ain’t easy. Furthermore, it is a life-long process.


Then how is it that we adults are so shocked at the resistance kids put up to controlling their strong and wild impulses? How is it that we are so intolerant when they don’t succeed? How is it that while we’re struggling with controlling our own impulses we are so quick to pathologize “impulse control” in children and label them? How is it that we drug our children to numb and paralyze their impulses instead of teaching them how to channel them? Could it be that we ourselves have very little impulse control?! (And sure enough, a lot of us do self-medicate through our various addictions to pacify our own impulses.)


I have wondered about this for a long time – and I will continue to wonder. But what I want to bring up here is that manners are a gentle and elegant way of learning impulse control.


There was a news item linked on this site a while ago that I particularly appreciated. It was about a school in London where children are served good food and are expected to observe good manners at the table, serving themselves and others with calm and respect. What a brilliant idea! I especially liked it that serving good food was coupled with the idea of having manners. After all, how can we expect our children to observe table manners if their food is slapped in front of them in barely-defrosted, packaged globs?


Imagine the lunch room in the average public school. Kids screaming and shoving, throwing the inedible garbage that they are given under the table or at each other… Lunch staff yanking seconds out of kids’ hands and lunch-room monitors screeching at the top of their lungs... Impulse control?! Who displays it for the children? And I don’t blame the adults trapped in that situation either. The problem is with the environment. Children and adults are thrown into an environment that brings out the worst out of all of them. It is an environment that makes it hard to coontrol the urge to scream, grab, elbow others out of the way, and just give up any attempt at decency. 


Now imagine if the lunch room were a place where the food itself was chosen with respect for children’s health. Imagine decent food served on real plates with real silverware and napkins. Imagine teachers sitting down with the children to eat. Imagine the children having to say “May I have the water?” if they’re thirsty. Imagine them passing the bread to each other and clearing the table attentively so they don’t break the dishes. Wouldn’t all this teach impulse control?
