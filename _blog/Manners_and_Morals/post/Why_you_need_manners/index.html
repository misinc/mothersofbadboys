Manners and Morals

Why you need manners

Sunday, October 10, 2010

By George Moore

 

Manners are patterns of social behavior. Good manners are the actions of a courteous person; they are what a courteous person does. Courtesy is the practice of compassion, and good manners flow from the practice of courtesy.


Some people think you don’t need manners—it’s enough to be nice and generous. I’m sure this is true if you’re always nice and generous, but for me, distracted and self-centered as I am, it helps to have a reminder.


Some people go further and claim that you should say what you think at all times, that a white lie is hypocrisy and hypocrisy is wrong, that honesty is best even when it’s brutal. (People usually say this just after they have said something particularly unkind.)


Manners follow prescribed forms, and good manners include a personal touch tailored to the specific situation. Consider a thank you note. The simplest form is a statement of thanks. The personal touch is telling how the gift affects the recipient personally, and it can go on to acknowledge the donor’s efforts.


Suppose your son’s grandmother sends a birthday gift he doesn’t particularly like. Maybe it’s the wrong color or it doesn’t fit or it’s not a style that his friends wear. You prevail upon him to write a thank you note. He says it would be hypocritical to thank her and comes up with this: “Thank you for the sweater for my birthday. It’s too bad it’s ugly and out of fashion. I know you meant well, but if I wear it my friends will make fun of me. Next time maybe you should just send money so I can pick out something myself.” I’m sure your mother will be charmed. (“But next time we’ll both be happier,” he argues. “I’ll get something I like, my thanks will be sincere, and she’ll be glad she pleased me.")

Or he says, “Oh, that’s what grandmas do. I don’t have to thank her!” After all, we don’t thank a blanket for warming us up.


Or he’s tired and postpones writing a note. Or he doesn’t feel particularly thankful the day it arrives and wants to wait until he does, and time passes, until the obligation is forgotten.


But after all, she went to the store and searched for something and paid for it and wrapped it and sent it, thinking of him all the while. Even a brutally honest person can say: “Thank you for the sweater and the trouble you took to find it! It’s very warm and I can’t wait to wear it! You’re such a great grandma!”  (Unless he really doesn’t think she’s great—but can’t he find something great about her?)


My son asked why he should thank the birthday boy as he leaves the party. After all, Alex invited him for his own pleasure, the pleasure of having friends celebrate his birthday, and the pleasure of receiving gifts. Shouldn’t Alex thank him? Certainly, I said, but you thank him for being liked enough to be invited, for the fun you had because he was there, for the trouble he and his parents took to organize the party. Thanking him gives him pleasure by showing that you enjoyed his company.


Good manners are based on consideration of other people’s feelings; they’re not based on what you feel.
